The journalist steps onto the submarine with the quiet satisfaction of a man who has finally caught the break he's been chasing. For years, he's scratched at the edges of strange stories—whispers of ghost ships, unexplained disappearances, government submarines departing from hidden ports—but nothing ever stuck. Now this: an exclusive invitation to document the first ever dimensional voyage beneath the sea. A legitimate miracle of science. A frontier no human has crossed. He clutches his notebook like a lifeline, already drafting the opening lines in his head, already imagining the awards, the recognition, the doors this will open. He has no idea what's waiting below.

The submarine descends through water so dark it seems to swallow the light from the observation windows. The wealthy guests sip champagne and press their faces to the glass, giddy with the exclusivity of it all. They've paid fortunes for this—to be the first, to be the ones who can say they were there. The journalist moves among them, jotting observations, capturing quotes, noting the way the light plays across their faces as the last traces of the sun disappear. In the galley, a teenage sou chef wipes down counters and dreams of one day cooking for people who actually notice. He doesn't notice the crew's averted eyes either. No one does. No one wonders about the lower decks, the sealed hatches, the compartments not marked on any map. They're all too busy with the story they think they're living.
Then the captain's voice crackles over the intercom.

"Ladies and gentlemen, we are approaching the threshold. In approximately sixty seconds, this vessel will become the first in human history to cross into another dimension. I want to thank you all for being part of this journey. Now—raise your glasses. Here's to the unknown."
The guests erupt in applause. Glasses rise. Strangers embrace. Someone starts a countdown. Ten. Nine. Eight. The journalist scribbles furiously, capturing every detail. The sou chef peers through the galley doorway, watching the wealthy elite celebrate, and allows himself a small smile. Seven. Six. Five. The lights flicker. Four. Three. Two. The ship lurches. One.
Silence.

Then the lights return, and the guests cheer. They clap each other on the back. They cry. They laugh. They raise their glasses again, toasting their own bravery, their own luck, their own place in history. The journalist writes it all down—the triumph, the tears, the way the woman beside him kisses her husband like they've survived a war.
They have no idea what's already inside them.

The green light starts as a glow beneath the floor panels. Then it spreads—up the walls, across the ceiling, through the observation windows where the void outside now pulses with something that might be alive. The applause dies in throats. Glasses shatter. Someone screams as the light touches them and their hand keeps going, twisting, lengthening, splitting into things that were not fingers a moment ago
Then the lights go out.

Absolute darkness. Not the kind your eyes adjust to—the kind that presses in, that has weight, that moves. In the black, you hear the first bite. The wet tear. The scream that becomes a gurgle that becomes something else entirely.
And then the screaming stops, and the feeding begins.

The emergency lights flicker to life—dim, red, wrong—and you see what the darkness was hiding. The observation lounge is a slaughterhouse. But worse than the blood, worse than the bodies, is what's still moving among them.
A woman who kissed her husband moments ago now has a face that opens sideways, and her husband is inside her, still twitching, still becoming. His arm, half-consumed, reaches out from between her teeth as if still searching for something to hold.
At a nearby table, a man who laughed at the countdown now lies writhing on the floor. His skin has turned slick and scaly, catching the red light like oil on water. From his torso, heavy sacs have bloomed—pulsing, translucent things filled with milky fluid that shifts and swells. They drag him down, pin him to the ground, a punishment made flesh for a life of taking what was not offered. He crawls helplessly, unable to rise, unable to do anything but leak into the carpet.
Across the room, a woman's head opens. Not her skull—something growing from it. A box. A screen. Glass and static pushing through bone as her real face recedes inside, disappearing into the glow. Her body fuses to the wall behind her, spine merging with bulkhead, and her new face flickers to life—showing everything. The feeding. The fleeing. The dying. She sees it all. She could warn someone. She could move. But she spent her whole life watching. She doesn't know how to stop.
Others have become something else entirely. Hulking things, bellowing, their flesh rotting even as it moves, sea-creature horrors that drag guests down and tear them apart with jaws that unhinge too wide. They hunt. They chase. They are wrath made meat, and they are fast.

The ones who carried the heaviest sins have become almost unrecognizable—bodies folded into new shapes, new purposes, new hungers. The fishmonger with his endless, punishing seed, pinned and helpless. The watcher with her screen-face, seeing all and doing nothing. The wrathful beasts that hunt in packs. They are what they always were, now made manifest.
And then there are the others. The ones who haven't changed at all.

They stand frozen in the chaos, untouched by mutation, their faces pale with terror. A few heartbeats pass. The creatures notice them. The pure ones don't transform. They don't become monsters. They just... scream. And then they're gone, torn apart by the very people they once shared champagne with.
Purity, in this place, is not protection. It's a target.
You press yourself against a bulkhead, clutching your notebook. You haven't changed. Neither has the boy beside you—the sou chef from the galley, no older than seventeen, his face smudged with kitchen grease. You don't know why you're both still human. You don't have time to wonder.
He grabs your arm and pulls.

You follow because you have nothing else. Because he's still human. Because in this nightmare, that means something.
He drags you through hatches, through service passages, through corridors the wealthy never see. The ship is changing around you. The walls are no longer just metal. They glisten. They pulse. Veins thread through the bulkheads, and what might have been wiring now looks disturbingly like sinew. The floor beneath your feet has a give to it, a warmth, as if you're running across something alive. Pipes weep fluids that might be condensation or might be something else entirely. Every surface seems to breathe.
Behind you, the sounds of pursuit grow louder. Closer.
You reach a heavy bulkhead door. The sou chef slams his palm against the release. Nothing. He hits it again. The mechanism is dead, jammed, changed. In the dim light, you can see why.
The door's edges are no longer clean steel. They're rimmed with something that looks like wet cartilage, soft and glistening. The release mechanism itself has partially transformed—a metal handle half-absorbed into puckered flesh, buttons replaced by what might be vertebrae, small and wet and wrong. The door isn't broken. It's becoming. It has grown its own seal, its own stubborn life, and it will not open for you.
The howls are just around the corner now.

The sou chef looks at the door. Looks at you. His eyes are calm. Clear. Certain.
He doesn't speak. He doesn't hesitate.

He turns and walks back toward the corridor you just came from.
You reach for him, try to grab his arm, but he's already moving, already committed. He steps through the hatchway back into the darkness, back toward the sounds of tearing flesh and clicking claws. And then—with a strength you wouldn't have expected—he pulls the heavy bulkhead door shut behind him.
The metal slams into place. The fleshy growth around the edges squelches as it seals, organic matter fusing with steel, completing what the mutation began. The lock engages from the other side.
He sealed you in.
He locked himself out.

You hear the sounds through the door. The creatures reaching him. The wet impact. The silence that follows.
He was pure. That's why he didn't change. That's why he helped you without a moment's thought, without a word, without any expectation of return. That's why he's dead now, torn apart by things that were people, because his goodness made him vulnerable, because purity in this place is just another word for prey.
And then, impossibly, you hear something else—a single, quiet thump against the metal. Not a claw. Not a demand. Just... a hand. Pressing once against the corrupted surface. A farewell. A job done.
Then nothing.

You stand there in the darkness, heart pounding, breath ragged. The storage room is pitch black—no emergency lights, no glow, nothing but the sound of your own breathing and the distant, wet pulse of the ship around you.
Your hand finds the device on your belt. You press the activation.
With a soft hum, a small sphere lifts from its housing and drifts into the air beside you. It glows—a warm, floating light that casts long shadows across the corrupted walls. A drone-light. Futuristic. Your only companion now.
The beam cuts through the dark, illuminating the storage room in fragments. Crates. Supplies. A hallway beyond, leading deeper into the ship.
You take a breath. You step forward.
